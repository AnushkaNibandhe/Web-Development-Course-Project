Humayun's Tomb

Humayun's Tomb, built in 1570, is a magnificent example of Mughal architecture and was the first garden-tomb in the Indian subcontinent. Commissioned by Emperor Humayunâ€™s wife, Empress Bega Begum, and designed by Persian architect Mirak Mirza Ghiyas, the tomb set a precedent for future Mughal mausoleums, including the iconic Taj Mahal. The structure, made of red sandstone with white marble inlays, stands at the center of a Charbagh (four-part) garden, symbolizing the Islamic concept of paradise. The tomb complex also houses the graves of other Mughal royals, making it a significant site of historical and cultural importance. Its architectural features, such as the double dome and the harmonious proportions, reflect a blend of Persian, Central Asian, and Indian influences. The site was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993 and is celebrated for its architectural innovation and historical significance.
